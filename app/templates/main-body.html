Long before David Hess died at the hands of staffers at Wordsworth, there were clear signs of trouble at the West Philadelphia facility.

In 2015, police say, three girls who lived there were sexually assaulted by a counselor who lured them with promises of money and gifts. While rumors of the incidents swirled, Wordsworth officials were slow to investigate, and the girls say the assaults continued for weeks.

Earlier that year, a boy in the program was sexually assaulted by another teen who was supposed to be kept under constant supervision. When the victim initially reported the crime, staffers were dismissive, he said, and he had to beg to be taken to the hospital. An exam there found tears in his anus.

When he returned to the facility, he was moved to another wing, where he later told police he was assaulted a second time by a different attacker. Terrified, he ran away.

In the past decade, police have been summoned to Wordsworth more than 800 times, records show, for incidents ranging from minor disturbances and tripped fire alarms to rape. In all, more than four dozen sex crimes were reported at the center in those years, including 10 rapes, according to police records obtained by the Inquirer.

This violence unfolded inside a little-known treatment center that was the only one of its kind in Philadelphia — and for many of the troubled young people who lived there, a place of last resort. Taxpayers shelled out $119,000 a year for the care of each child but all too often reaped failure.

Last fall, Wordsworth added a more serious crime to its roster: homicide.

The city Medical Examiner's Office ruled that Hess, 17, was suffocated by staffers who went to his room in search of a stolen iPod and struggled with him when he grew agitated as they flipped over his bed and tossed furniture around his room.

At one point, a staffer held him down while another punched him repeatedly in the ribs, witnesses later told state investigators.

"Get off me," he cried, according to a state report on the incident. "I can't breathe."

And then, witnesses said, the room fell silent.

"He died a child," said Hess's sister, Elizabeth, 28. "He died a child in a facility designed to help him, and it didn't help him, it killed him."

Days after Hess' Oct. 13 death, the state Department of Human Services ordered Wordsworth to close, saying it posed "an immediate and serious danger to the health and safety of the residents."

That decision — which Wordsworth has appealed — came years after repeated warnings from state inspectors, lawyers and others who found conditions there alarming.

"It's horrible," said Jonathan Cohen, a lawyer for the 16-year-old boy who was raped. "They're supposed to be protecting these kids and keeping them safe."

A former Wordsworth resident who spent six years at the facility said she'd watched staffers harm children with impunity during her time there.

"I seen people get broken arms. I see people punch a girl straight in the face. Nothing happened," she said. "Since we ain't got no family, they look at us like we ain't s—. We ain't got no fancy parents. Our parents is crackheads or mental."

On the sprawling grounds of a former hospital, Wordsworth runs a variety of programs that aim to help children and families in need. Among them is the residential treatment facility where Hess drew his last breath.

Hess, who suffered from profound mental illness, was among 82 young people at Wordsworth on that October night. Many had been sent there by the city after suffering abuse or neglect. Others were ordered to stay there after committing a crime. All had mental or behavioral problems.

Ranging in age from six to 21, the young people in Wordsworth's care lived in a rundown facility and slept in rooms with holes in the walls, exposed wiring, broken light fixtures and faulty heaters. There, records show, they were sometimes tended to by ill-trained staffers.

Over the years, inspectors from the state Department of Human Services repeatedly cited the facility for failing to ensure that its workers had the required training in the care of and management of children. Wordsworth officials repeatedly pledged to be more diligent.

Yet state records show that two of the staffers involved in the fatal confrontation with Hess had not had the required annual training in the proper methods of restraining a child and thus, did not meet state requirements for working with children.

"It's like carrying a gun without a license. You either have it or you don't" said Steven F. Marino, a lawyer for the Hess family who plans to sue Wordsworth. "You're either allowed to be around children or you're not."

Officials at Wordsworth declined to be interviewed and declined to respond to a detailed list of questions from the Inquirer.

In a statement, Wordsworth's board chairman, Thomas V. Johnson said: "At Wordsworth, there is no greater value we have than the responsibility to care for our children." He said many of the young people who live there "had experienced exceptional hardships in their lives with behavioral and other mental health issues."

Johnson said pending lawsuits prevented him from discussing the incidents the Inquirer asked about. However, he said, "we would like to state unequivocally that Wordsworth remains committed to ensuring a safe and secure environment for all children in our care."

In a statement after the Medical Examiner's Office declared Hess' death a homicide, Johnson said he and others at Wordsworth were "deeply saddened" by the incident and pledged to cooperate fully as state and local agencies investigated.

He also noted that in January, Wordsworth hired a new chief executive officer, Diana Ramsay, and said that under her leadership, "We all remain fully committed to ensuring that Wordsworth continues to do what is at the core of our vital mission — to transform children's lives in positive and meaningful ways." Ramsay replaced Debra Lacks, who led Wordsworth for eight years before quietly leaving her post three months ago. Lacks declined to say whether her departure was voluntary, as did Johnson and other officials at Wordsworth. She declined to be interviewed about Hess' death and other events of her tenure.

Despite its deficiencies, Wordsworth, which opened in 2006, had its license renewed again and again, and child welfare agencies and the courts continued to send young people there.

"We did rely on Wordsworth extensively," said Joan Erney, chief executive of Community Behavioral Health, a nonprofit that oversees mental health services for the city. "Places outside of Philadelphia don't want to take our kids. They tell us our kids are too complicated. They tell us our kids are too hard. We have kids with some really difficult problems."

Wordsworth, the only residential treatment facility in the city, was hired to care for a steady stream of young people at an average cost of $326 per day. Wordsworth is licensed to handle 124 children, but its capacity varies, officials say.

CBH funnels about $14 million a year in state and federal money to Wordsworth. The agency continued to write those checks even after it learned of serious problems at the facility, including the sexual assaults.

"Our approach to agencies generally is that we need them, and if there are opportunities to improve, we work with them," said Erney. "In the case of Wordsworth, there were challenges.

Two years before the state ordered Wordsworth to shut down, CBH, alerted by concerned lawyers at the Defender Association of Philadelphia, began holding monthly meetings with officials at the facility.

Also at the meetings were representatives of the city and Department of Human Services, which is responsible for the care of children who have been abused or neglected or have been adjudicated delinquent after committing crimes; the state Department of Human Services, which licenses and inspects the facility; lawyers from the Defender Association; and representatives of Philadelphia's juvenile probation department.

Erney said she believed Wordsworth had been making strides. Officials there agreed to put cameras in the building and fix up conditions inside, she said. They also pledged to improve training.

The monthly meetings became bi-monthly meetings.

"Everyone seemed to think that they were on the right path, and we were going to go to every six months" for meetings, Erney said.

In retrospect, she said, CBH officials erred in not reading the state inspection reports on Wordsworth. Had they done so, they would have learned of lingering problems, including lapses in training, delays in medical care and injuries to children through improper restraints.

In the report ordering Wordsworth's closure days after Hess' death, state officials cited "gross incompetence, negligence and misconduct in operating the facility."

The assessment came nearly a year after three girls, ages 15 to 17, reported repeated sexual assaults by residential counselor Isaac Outten. The girls said Outten took them to the basement for sex and forced them to take naked photographs of themselves with his iPhone.

Police say he lured the 15-year-old to the basement by promising her diapers and milk for her 1-year-old child in exchange for oral sex and intercourse. They say he promised another girl, 17, that he would help her with a criminal case in exchange for sex and naked selfies, police said.

And the third girl, also 17, told police Outten led her to the basement for sex after a counselor left her alone with him. She said he told her not to tell anyone or he would get into trouble.

Wordsworth suspended and later fired Outten after one of the girls told a counselor what happened. But some staffers had heard rumblings that Outten was having sex with one or more of the girls and they did nothing, according to a summary of interviews Wordsworth officials conducted in preparation for the defense of a lawsuit.

Outten, 37, was charged with institutional sexual assault, involuntary deviate sexual intercourse, corruption of minors, child endangerment and other crimes and is awaiting trial. He has pleaded not guilty and is awaiting trial.

Nadeem A. Bezar, a lawyer who has sued Wordsworth on behalf of two of the girls, said the center breached its duty to them by allowing Outten to be alone with them and thus, failing to protect them.

"It is unfortunate that Wordsworth did not follow protocol that would have prevented their employee from preying on these vulnerable girls," he said. He also faulted officials at Wordsworth for not investigating the rumors that Outten was having sex with the girls.

"The failure of the staff to report what was known at Wordsworth is upsetting," he said. "They should have known. People were talking in the hallways."

That three girls in a single facility could be repeatedly assaulted by a staffer stunned many in the child welfare community.

"I couldn't believe that. It's pretty outrageous," said Frank Cervone, executive director of the Center for Child Advocates, which represents victims of child abuse and neglect.

"Two occasions, three occasions, four occasions," he said, quoting a state report that substantiated the girls' accounts of the assaults and faulted Wordsworth for failing to keep them safe. "I don't think any of us in the advocacy community were aware of this at all."

Cynthia Figueroa, commissioner of the city's Department of Human Services, was similarly upset both by reports of the sexual assaults and of the boy's death.

"David's death was unacceptable," she said. "The manner of his death was troubling.… and it was concerning to know that there had been previous egregious behavior by the staff."

Figueroa was new to the job when Hess was killed at Wordsworth and she hurried to the center to investigate.

"I wanted to see it with my own eyes," she said.

What she found shocked her. The place was in "horrible condition," she said.

"It was not the conditions conducive to a child that would be in our care," she said. "It was really worn down."

The violent struggle that killed Hess was not the first time staffers at Wordsworth went too far in trying to restrain a child. Nor was it the last.

Interviews, court records and state inspection reports show a trail of injuries to children, including broken bones. Along the way, there were repeated warnings that staffers were not using approved methods for safely restraining a child. And there were repeated calls for improved training.

Proper training in how to safely restrain a child is essential in facilities such as Wordsworth, where children can grow combative quickly, experts say.

In Hess' case, he had psychiatric disorders that made him prone to anger and violence, said Marino, the lawyer for his family. "You're going to have to restrain him," he said. "It's foreseeable. But they're not trained to properly restrain the child so they use techniques they'd use in the street."
